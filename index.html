Coming from nowhere?

Fog shows up when water vapor, or water in its gaseous form, condenses. During condensation, molecules of water vapor combine to make tiny liquid water droplets that hang in the air. We can see fog because of these tiny water droplets. Water vapor, a gas, is invisible.
Fog happens when it’s very, very humid. There has to be a lot of water vapor in the air for fog to form. In order for fog to form, dust or some kind of air pollution needs to be in the air. Water vapor condenses around these microscopic solid particles.

Most common types of fog

Radiation Fog

Common both at night and dawn, sometimes being called morning fog, radiation fog forms when the ground cools, subsequently cooling surrounding air to its dew-point. This fog tends to "burn-off" in the morning as the sun re-heats the ground and air.

Advection Fog

When moist air moves over cooler ground or water, advection fog forms. It's most common along the coast, as sea breeze blows moist air over land.This is why it is being called sea fog.

Precipitation Fog

As warm rain falls through cool air, the precipitation saturates the air. Mixed in with rain, it can be dense and long-lasting thus making it tough to determine exactly where precipitation fog is ... or isn't.

Upslope Fog

Upslope fog or hill fog forms when winds blow air up a slope - moist air rises up terrain and condenses into clouds. The air cools as it rises, allowing moisture in it to condense. Upslope fog is common on mountain slopes and passes.

Steam Fog

Common during cold weather months over bodies of water, steam fog forms when cold and dry air moves over warm water - kind of like advection fog in reverse. It is much more shallow and less dense than radiational, advection, or upslope fog.

Freezing Fog

Freezing fog is a subtype of other types of fog. It occurs when the temperature is below freezing but the cloud droplets remain in a liquid state. Freezing fog can quickly glaze a thin layer of black ice over roadways and runways, turning them into an ice rink within a matter of seconds.
